3) The hard part: “new vape on top of old pile” image logic
You nailed the failure mode: every “after” photo includes history.

Best practical solution that keeps compute cheap:

Take a BEFORE photo right before allowing the user to drop (or right when sensor detects presence at the chute)

Take an AFTER photo immediately after impact

Compute a difference crop:

Align images (small perspective shift)

Pixel difference mask

Find the largest new region (the new object)

Crop to that bounding box and send only the crop to the classifier

Store both original images anyway for audits and appeals

This approach is fast, explainable, and gets you higher accuracy than trying to segment “the newest object” from a single cluttered image.

4) AI backend that is cost efficient and doesn’t look janky to investors
You basically have 3 tiers:

Tier A: Buy tokens (recommended for v1 and investor demos)

Pros: fastest to ship, best accuracy quickly, no ML ops overhead

Cons: per image cost, vendor dependence

Architecture: your server uploads crop image to AI API, gets JSON back

Tier B: Hybrid, tokens plus “local memory”

You still call an AI vision model, but you reduce cost by:

cropping tightly (massive savings)

caching by image hash

using embeddings to match known products first

only calling “heavy” classification when similarity is low

This is the sweet spot: cheap, scalable, impressive

Tier C: Own servers

Pros: potentially cheaper at huge scale, full control

Cons: expensive to build, maintain, and keep accurate; you will spend your life doing ML babysitting

Only makes sense once volume is high and your labels are strong

My strong suggestion: Tier B for your “serious startup” story.

Cloud vision for classification + embeddings

Your own database becomes the moat: labeled drops, brand subtype flavor taxonomy, appeals, corrections
